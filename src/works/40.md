# description

Authors of experimental literature have explored the potential of many textual interfaces, from virtual reality to codex books, but less attention has been given to one of the environments in which text is most often encountered: text-editing software.

*The Writer's Project* is a series of playful text-editors-as-texts. These are editors that intervene in the act of writing in unexpected, challenging, and unusual ways. Some of the editors are conceptual experiments in user interface design: What if the text editor asked you if you were sure before committing to each word? What if the delete key was not functional? Others are disruptive aids built as creative writing tools (such an editor that refracts words through different thesauri, giving word choice ideas), or interactive visual poems inspired by past concrete and kinetic poetry. Embedded in many of the experiments are questions about the texture of cybernated wordplay, or concerns about the role of the page in forming identity. This project avoids LLM use despite editors' insistence on interfering with grammar and diction, taking loose aim at the conditions of AI-mediated writing, built to create a tension between writerly agency and the systems' resistance.

As AI writing tools seem to devalue the written word, this project is an attempt to think about the performativity already inherent in digital text editors, a call for us to experience the text editor as an authored instance of time-based media whose design decisions enact arguments. As such, playing the pieces (writing with them) is meant to engage in issues of kinetic poetry as well as critical AI discourses.

To showcase the editors from the *Writer's Project, 'Drifts'* is a guided experience through a scripted sequence of the editors. A number of 'Drifts' are available, each a collection of curated paths and writing prompts that guide the player-writer through a progression of the editors. At the end of each Drift sequence, players have the option of printing a chapbook-the pages they wrote through the Drift.

Hopefully this (growing) collection is a provocative, fun, and sometimes useful tool for producing unusual writing.

# curatorialStatement

*Drifts* reconceives writing not as the production of stable text but as an ongoing negotiation with the affordances and resistances of digital tools. Each writing environment establishes its own constraints, rhythms, and possibilities, inviting participants to discover how interface design actively shapes creative thought rather than merely recording it. Authorship emerges as a dialogue between writer and system, where composition becomes a process of experimentation instead of straightforward inscription.

The work extends a rich tradition within electronic literature that treats software as a co-creative partner. By introducing procedural transformations, shifting linguistic relationships, and unexpected interventions into the act of writing, *Drifts* foregrounds the generative potential of computational mediation. The resulting texts are less significant as finished artefacts than as traces of encounters between intention, algorithmic behaviour, and chance.

Equally compelling as a creative practice and a pedagogical resource, *Drifts* encourages reflection on writing as an embodied interaction with tools whose assumptions often remain invisible. In making those assumptions explicit—and delightfully unstable—it demonstrates how electronic literature can function simultaneously as artwork, instrument, and critical inquiry into the technologies through which language is produced.

# creatorBiography

**Alex Calderwood** is a PhD student, researcher, programmer, and language artist. He is pursuing a PhD in Computational Media at UC Santa Cruz, advised by Noah Wardrip-Fruin and Michael Mateas. His current work is in practice-based research, where he builds text editors that facilitate critical inquiry and wordplay. He is also a published poet (with an upcoming concrete poetry chapbook), and has formally worked as a journalist and AI researcher.
