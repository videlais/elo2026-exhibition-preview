# description

*Get Lost* is a hypertextual poetic journal project. It is a work-in-progress in which I use web development as a writing tool, with the idea that the programming technique is roughly the same since the beginning of the Internet. This kind of technical timelessness of digital technology (simple HTML code, CSS, and JavaScript) intersects with the reflections and ideas recorded in this journal, which evokes my daily life in a very current reality, in terms of the social and political situation and the ideological divisions that arise from it. My very personal relationship with artistic creation is intertwined with the more general observation that we are living in complex times, where violence, hatred, injustice, and the destruction of ecosystems seem almost inevitable.  

The title *Get Lost* evokes ideological confrontation, rejection of others, but more broadly, the idea of losing oneself, which is both a source of renewal, according to the situationist theory (that is to say, Guy Debord and the Situationist International movement), but also a labyrinth from which one must try to escape, despite everything.  

The current version is only a prototype. The appearance and aesthetics will be worked on using CSS. But the basic idea of a non-linear journal is already in place. The use of hyperlinks will, in a way, create a second narrative that links the pages together, while gradually enhancing the first level of narrative. Eventually, I plan to add images (mainly calligraphy and drawing). This will allow me to highlight the act of scribbling, writing by hand, and drawing. I want to incorporate traces of these gestures as elements of performance that are integrated into the narrative of the journal. These images will allow the audience to vary the pace of viewing and reading. I also use a few emojis, because they are an integral part of web culture, and because they are emblematic of the relationship with images in contemporary communication.

Some generative AI (mostly Gemini) has been used to help in the coding of the piece.

Even though the project is mainly in English, it also contains parts in French and Kréyol gwadloupèyen. As a Québécois (French Canadian) who lived in France for a while then got married to someone from Gwadloup in the West Indies, code switching and the blending of languages and cultures is part of my life. I decided to keep this multilingual mix in the work instead of translating everything, in order to better represent this reality. The semantic ambiguity is therefore an integral part of the work. Furthermore, colonialism is intrinsically hidden beneath linguistic issues, and I did not want a complete translation to mask this aspect.  

# curatorialStatement

*Get Lost* interweaves personal journal entries with news-related stories to build a labyrinthine maze of loops and combinatorial poetic forms. Drawing on the author's own reflections alongside observations of violence and injustice, the work holds the reader within a cycle of semantic ambiguity, shifting between English, French, and Kréyol gwadloupèyen. This movement across languages unsettles both the reader's position within the work and their grasp of the text itself, producing a deliberate interpretational drift in which meaning is continually deferred.

That disorientation is enacted at the level of structure. Composed as a collection of linked HTML files, the work circulates a repeating set of phrases, moving the reader from static passages into moments of dynamic, shifting text and disrupting the convention of the single, self-contained interactive page. In doing so, *Get Lost* makes navigation itself a form of meaning-making, offering a reflection on how multilingual, combinatorial writing can render the experience of dislocation as something the reader must move through rather than resolve.

# creatorBiography

**Yan St-Onge** is an intermedia artist, poet, and performer. He holds a PhD in semiotics from the Université du Québec à Montréal. He also graduated from the École Supérieure des Beaux-Arts de Marseille. At the intersection of visual arts, digital arts, and literary arts, his creative work has been shown in Canada, France, Portugal, and on the web in the form of exhibitions, images, projections, performances, texts, and sound works. His poems and texts have been published in various journals. His performance work combines performance art, digital arts, poetry, and sound poetry. In recent years, his creative work has been influenced by conceptual practices, electronic literature, interactive poetry, and digital culture. Over the past two years, he has been working on a research project focusing on the use of AI in the creative process.
