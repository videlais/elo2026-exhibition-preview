# description

*Mystery Fragments* is an endless animation cycling between two scenes: a cat and dog talking about the mysterious disappearance of their human providers, and the cryptic television program they're watching on TV. The dialog between the cat and dog is partly scripted, and inspired in part by "Endgame", by Samuel Beckett, a play set in post-apocalyptic time, with characters waiting for an ending that never arrives. One line in the cycle is generated from "The Gnosis of the Light" a section from the Untitled Apocalypse contained in the Codex Brucianus, an early Gnostic text, using a context free grammar approach. The result is a contrast two takes on eschatology, a theme I find to be more and more prevalent in the thinking of current political and philosophical movements, exploring how and why we imagine the end of the world. The dialog system generates a simple, cyclical narrative, that swaps components over time, maintaining its rhythm while losing its sense. The music is procedurally generated variations of two themes, which will develop and change while the animation loops. "Mystery Fragments" was published in the Spring 2026 issue of "The New River Journal."

# curatorialStatement

Presented as a reflection on eschatology, *Mystery Fragments* combines an endless animation cycle of a cat and dog, partially scripted lines drawn from Samuel Beckett's *Endgame* and selections of Gnostic texts, and procedurally generated chant to stage a paradoxical, cyclical narrative: one fixed on an end that never comes. Interaction triggers a beginning without conclusion, so that every beginning and ending originates with the user.

Pairing ecclesiastical chant with the mundane scene of a cat and dog, *Mystery Fragments* evokes an unnerving present searching for an end that never arrives. Though limited in interactivity, the work turns the wait for input and the absence of output into its own subject, sustaining an ever-drawn, interstitial state that drifts between frames of animation and reference even as the audio morphs over time. In doing so, the work offers a reflection on how digital media can hold a narrative permanently suspended between beginning and end.

# creatorBiography

**Owen Roberts** (b. 1985, Burlington, Vermont, USA) is an artist and educator based in Brooklyn, New York. His work consists of animation, art games and other multimedia works. He builds software tools to synthesize coding and multimedia with research, writing and drawing practices, resulting in a characteristic style and voice influenced by cartoons, early 3d video games, glitch aesthetics, theatre of the absurd, religious texts, symbolism, the occult, and whatever recent rabbit-hole he has descended into. Owen is a faculty member in the Media Arts and Technology Department at the Borough of Manhattan Community College. His website is [https://owen.cool](https://owen.cool).
