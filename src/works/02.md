# description

*Digital T\[H\]Readings \[1994-2025\]* is an interactive browser-native archive-work that treats documentation as a literary form: authored, navigable, and meant to be read rather than merely referenced. Structured as an expandable chronology, it invites viewers to traverse three decades of electronic literature and internet art through dense year-by-year nodes. Each entry pairs core metadata (medium, context, collaborators) with an "About" layer that performs a compact critical narration: how a work functioned in its original technocultural moment, what kinds of networked communities carried it, and what remains when those conditions dissolve.

Where many archives hide breakdown, Digital T\[H\]Readings keeps it visible. Dead links, vanished hosts, deprecated formats, shifting browser standards, and platform migration are surfaced as part of the record, turning obsolescence into a readable condition rather than a silent technical failure. In the ELO2026 context of (Un)Supervised, the work foregrounds how digital culture is continually "supervised" by commercial infrastructures and automated gatekeeping, while preservation and access-making often happen through unglamorous, human-scale labor: tending, narrating, re-linking, and re-situating.

The interface supports both linear traversal and associative drift, allowing history to be encountered as sequence or recombination. Embedded screenshots and contextual notes operate as micro-traversals, enabling encounter even when original environments no longer run. Digital T\[H\]Readings thus functions as both archive and artwork: a map of migrating networks and techno-communities, and a model for archiving electronic literature without de-authoring it. It asks readers to notice who gets excluded when access is supervised and how community persists through repair, not scale alone.

# curatorialStatement

*Digital T[H]Readings [1994–2025]* presents archiving as an ongoing creative and interpretive practice rather than a neutral act of documentation. Organised as an expandable chronology spanning three decades of digital works, collaborations, and networked projects, the archive situates each piece within its technological and cultural context through concise critical commentary, screenshots, and production details. Rather than concealing the fragility of born-digital media, it foregrounds broken links, obsolete platforms, and shifting technical standards as part of the historical record, revealing digital decay as an essential component of electronic literature's history.

The work is particularly valuable as a model of preservation that resists reducing digital artworks to static metadata. By documenting contexts of production, collaboration, and technological change alongside the works themselves, *Digital T[H]Readings* highlights the often invisible labour required to sustain access to electronic literature. It reminds us that preserving digital culture involves continual acts of maintenance, reinterpretation, and care, offering a thoughtful reflection on how the histories of networked art can remain legible long after their original technological environments have disappeared.

# creatorBiography

Mez Breeze is an Australian digital artist and writer whose innovative work has shaped electronic literature, net art, and interactive narrative since the 1990s. Renowned for creating *mezangelle*, a pioneering language that merges natural language and code, she expands the boundaries of digital storytelling and immersive art. Her projects encompass virtual reality (VR), augmented reality (AR), games, and AI art, including award-winning works like *All the Delicate Duplicates* and *Perpetual Nomads*. Mez's creations have been exhibited internationally and are featured in numerous academic publications. She has collaborated with leading technology companies and continues to influence the field through her exploration of synthetic environments and interactive projects.
