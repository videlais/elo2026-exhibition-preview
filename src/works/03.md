# description

*What Happened: Indexed Evidence* is a Twine hypertext memoir structured as the backmatter index of a book that does not exist. The work documents intimate partner abuse through the form of its aftermath: an index compiled in retrospect, in language built after survival rather than during it.

Navigation follows "see also" cross-references, replicating the associative, non-linear logic of trauma. Some paths resolve. Others terminate without exit. An input field accepts text and returns nothing. A second-person interrogation demands to know where you were and who you were with. Twine's linked, nonlinear architecture is the argument. The platform's passage-based structure, in which every node connects outward but no path leads to a recoverable center, formally enacts what the index's content describes: a system of cross-references that circle meaning without arriving at it. The form not only illustrates the experience of surviving abuse but also replicates its logic.

The piece draws on several intersecting frameworks in electronic literature and digital memoir. Scott Rettberg's Fin du Monde, a multimodal work published in The Digital Review combining AI-generated images and music to narrate a post-human apocalypse, introduces cyborg authorship as a framework for understanding how digital processes surface a cultural unconscious. This work grounds the project's treatment of survival language as data: indexed, cross-referenced, and ultimately irrecoverable in its totality.

Christine Wilks' Voices, an interactive digital fiction developed through the "Writing New Bodies" research project and exhibited at ELO 2023, withholds its subject's body entirely, generating meaning through deliberate absence. This work informs this piece's central formal choice that only the effects of what happened are indexed, never the event itself.

Lisa Farrell's "Daemons and Ghosts," an autotheoretical research log published in The Digital Review documenting AI collaboration on fiction, traces the experience of editing oneself into "only a character." That perspective shapes the autoethnographic logic here, where indexing becomes a technology of self-protection.

Laura Okkema's dissertation Hearing the Voices of the Deserters argues that hypertext authors exercise more control than print authors by coding every possible outcome, making constrained navigation a rhetorical force rather than a freedom. Such thinking drives the structural logic of this hypertext: every "see also" returns to the same place, and the reader who believes they are navigating freely is not.

The piece's formal argument is in the replication: abuse is structure. The entry that would explain everything is REDACTED. It was never indexed, but the reader searches for it anyway. The index that could not be written while it was happening is completed, finally, by a stranger clicking through it.

# curatorialStatement

*What Happened: Indexed Evidence* invites the reader into the backmatter of a book that does not exist. Presented as a set of cross-linked index entries, the work asks the reader to unravel a story by moving between fragments, piecing together a purposely withheld subject glimpsed only in glances.

Organised around the theme that "abuse is structure," the work sends the reader through seams of repeating patterns, (re)enacting the withheld past as a traversing present. In doing so, the reader becomes not only part of the hidden story but part of the structure itself, where the "abuse" is always "a stranger clicking through it": offering a reflection on how the act of reading can reproduce the very structures it seeks to expose.

# creatorBiography

**Nicole Patino** is a writer, educator, and graduate student at the University of Central Florida. Her work sits at the intersection of digital humanities, electronic literature, and creative nonfiction. She holds an MFA in Creative Nonfiction and teaches composition at Valencia College. Her creative and scholarly work explores trauma, form, and the limits of narrative.
