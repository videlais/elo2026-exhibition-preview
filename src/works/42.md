# description

This interactive literary work forms part of an ongoing series exploring speculative collaborations among authors, readers, texts, and machines across time. Bringing together public domain books, agricultural manuals, architectural guides, early cybernetic writing, obsolete software, and other historical artifacts, the series approaches these materials not as documents of the past but as unfinished conversations, where historical aspirations encounter new media and computational forms. Embedded within them are longings for comfort, beauty, cultivation, intelligence, cooperation, and transformation—desires that often outlive the conditions that first gave rise to them. As technologies, readers, and systems of knowledge pass into history, these aspirations are neither preserved nor abandoned, but translated into new material and imaginative contexts.

Sources for this work include late nineteenth and early twentieth-century publications such as *Woodward's Country Homes* (Geo. E. & F. W. Woodward, 1866) and *Amateur Gardencraft* (Eben E. Rexford, 1912). Illustrations from these books are transformed through successive encounters with an AI image-editing system, while the text emerges through an edited assemblage of archival fragments, my own writing, and passages generated by GPT-2, an obsolete language model that I run locally on my computer. Rather than reconstructing historical contexts, the work lingers in the intervals between them, where messages intended for one future arrive in another, and collaborators separated by generations and technologies briefly inhabit the same imaginative space.

This work extends a series of web-based literary artworks that combine archival materials with deliberately constrained generative AI. Previous works — including *Cicada Mountain*, *The home-life of the spoonbill, the stork and some herons*, *Pearls and Parasites*, and *Our Humble Helpers* — likewise assemble historical texts, archival imagery, and machine-generated language into interactive literary environments. Across the series, obsolete AI models serve not as imperfect predecessors of contemporary systems but as historically situated collaborators, participating alongside authors, translators, editors, and readers in conversations that unfold across multiple generations of media and technology.

# curatorialStatement

*Amateur Gardencraft* presents an "unfinished conversation" between two historical documents — *Woodward's Country Homes* (Geo. E. & F. W. Woodward, 1866) and *Amateur Gardencraft* (Eben E. Rexford, 1912), from which it takes its name — staging language models as interfaces between past authors and present readers. Purposely using an older language model, GPT-2, the work poses the question of where the past, present, and future modes of writing intersect, and whether the boundaries between generations can temporarily inhabit the same textual space as selections from the past fuel the transforming, visual present.

Through focusing on the friction between contexts, colonial natural history, the tonal quirks of an obsolete model, and contemporary language production, *Amateur Gardencraft* foregrounds anachronistic collisions rather than seamless prose, treating an outdated AI not as a rupture but as one more host in a long continuum of cross-technological dependency. The work offers a reflection on how past and present modes of writing can share a single textual space, holding written word and digital computation within the same fragile, generative relation.

# creatorBiography

**Daniel Lichtman** is an artist, researcher and community practitioner based in NYC. Lichtman's work explores how communities can use the tools of game design and visual art to tell stories about their culture and heritage, and imagine possible futures. Lichtman's framework for interactive storytelling, the Community Game Development Toolkit, is used by community members, artists and researchers internationally. Exhibitions and publications include Oral.Pub; ICA, London; Ammerman Center for Art and Technology; BRIC, The Bronx Museum and The Queens Museum. Lichtman is Assistant Professor, and Program Coordinator, of Game Development and Emerging Media at St. John's University, New York.
