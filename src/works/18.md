# description

*Actioncoderz* brings the code that is increasingly hidden in our everyday lives back into play. Change the code by commenting and uncommenting directly during execution to fix the games! The coder also becomes the target.

Normally, the hard-earned source code, with all its subtleties and comments, disappears forever during compilation, when the human-readable text becomes byte code that is extremely difficult to read in complex programs.

All the clever algorithms, custom-made products, and constructed software are no longer traceable. Some coders think this is perfect: it should just work — nothing more. And not everyone wants to reveal how simple their software is.

Actioncoderz brings code back into everyday life, placing it alongside its execution. The code is executed directly, and you can turn things on and off in the running (virtual) machine to solve or exacerbate problems.

Even a 'Hello World' program can become a challenge! Certain games take this even further: the code itself becomes a playground with twists and turns, and the coder can become the hunted.

In and-or's ActionCoderz, users can, of course, create their own puzzles and share them with others.

# curatorialStatement

*Actioncoderz* transforms programming from an invisible computational process into a site of direct interaction and play. Across a series of small challenges, participants manipulate executable code in real time, enabling and disabling lines to alter the behaviour of the program itself. Rather than treating source code as something hidden beneath an interface, the work places it at the centre of the experience, encouraging readers to engage with software as a dynamic, malleable text.

The collection occupies an intriguing space between game, programming environment, and electronic literature. Each puzzle invites experimentation, rewarding close attention to syntax, logic, and computational processes while introducing playful constraints that complicate straightforward execution. In doing so, *Actioncoderz* foregrounds code as both expressive medium and interactive material, demonstrating that programming languages can function not only as tools for creating digital works but also as sites of creative exploration in their own right. The result is an accessible reflection on the materiality of software, one that invites audiences to reconsider the relationship between author, reader, programmer, and machine through acts of playful intervention.

# creatorBiography

*Actioncoderz* was developed by the artgroup **AND-OR** (René Bauer and Beat Suter) in 2025. AND-OR is an artgroup specializing in media art and gameArt. The group consists of René Bauer, Beat Suter, and Mirjam Weder. The group has been operating since 2001 from Zurich, Switzerland, and has done more than 40 electronic artworks that are mostly of an experimental nature. See: https://www.and-or.ch/

**René Bauer** is a founding member of the artgroup AND-OR. He has an M.A. degree in German philology and literary studies, biology, and computer linguistics from the University of Zurich. Presently, he works at the Zurich University of the Arts (ZHdK) as a lecturer, researcher, and head of Master's education in the subject area of Game Design, where he also manages the GameLab together with Beat Suter. His interests encompass coding, game mechanics, game studies, art in/with games (https://www.and-or.ch/), and social media/knowledge systems (https://vintagecomputing.ch).

**Beat Suter**. His interests encompass coding, game mechanics, game studies, art in/with games (https://www.and-or.ch/), and social media/knowledge systems (https://vintagecomputing.ch). Beat Suter is a founding member of the artgroup AND-OR. He holds a doctorate in literature from the University of Zurich, with a focus on digital literature. He is a senior lecturer for Game Design at Zurich University of the Arts (ZHdK), Switzerland, specializing in concepts, storytelling, mechanics, and the history of games. He manages the GameLab together with René Bauer. Suter also works as an independent scholar and curator, and as editor and author with a special interest in narrative mechanics of games (www.cybersuter.ch).
