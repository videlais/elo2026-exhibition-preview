Italo Calvino's Invisible Cities And A Generative World

Description

Half a century ago, the Italian writer Italo Calvino asked: “Will I ever be able to say ‘it's writing today,' precisely as one says ‘it's raining today'?” In 2026, we find ourselves inhabiting the reality of that forecast; a new genre of writing and a new species of author have entered the literary realm: the machine—or more specifically, generative artificial intelligence. As an aesthetic response to this technological reality, our project presents a computational reimagining of Calvino's 1972 novel Invisible Cities and traces the genealogy of human-machine co-authorship back to his 1967 essay Cybernetics and Ghosts, which envisioned a combinatorial “literature machine” capable of exhausting every narrative permutation. Developed using multimodal Gemini models, this interactive prototype attempts to realize Calvino's vision of automatic writing within the infinite corridors of modern liminal space aesthetics. The system mirrors the dialogic structure of Calvino's novel, in which the Mongol emperor Kublai Khan commissions the Venetian traveler Marco Polo to describe the cities he has encountered on his journey. In our prototype, the user assumes the role of the emperor and commissions a generative AI—our algorithmic Marco Polo—to construct infinite liminal architectures of the modern world. The interface then translates these textual descriptions into visual imagery of contemporary liminal space aesthetics. The reader-user can then navigate the cities they have co-created within a dynamic 3D web environment, materializing the act of reading into an exploration, a wandering through a physical liminal space. The dialogic structure also serves as a direct tribute to ELIZA, the world's first conversational computer program. Through this setup, we hope modern readers can experience the birth of a collaborative, distributed authorship: one that ricochets, like a ping-pong ball, between human and machine.
