Like a Mountain of Sleep

Description

Like a Mountain of Sleep (LaMoS) is a browser-based interactive text built in the SugarCube Twine story format that systematically captures visitor text input and recontextualizes it as dialogue for threatening characters or as evidence of violence the visitor never intended. Visitors proceed under the assumption they are co-authoring the narrative; they discover instead that their contributions have been repositioned within frames they did not choose, rendering Foucault's author-function experiential rather than theoretical.

The work operates as a constant loss state, refusing narrative resolution or satisfying win conditions. Navigation between passages is randomized rather than authored, denying the directional logic that hypertext typically promises. The central mechanic, which the work calls "involuntary authorship," captures visitor responses to escalating prompts and reinserts them unpredictably into subsequent passages as elements of a spring break slasher movie that never existed. An input detection system actively rejects evasion attempts as keyboard mashing or crude deflection triggers escalating menace rather than silent acceptance.

Visual deterioration runs continuously in the background: film grain cycles at 24 frames per second to simulate projected footage, scanlines intensify across the viewport, and text becomes progressively corrupted with stacking diacritical marks (zalgo text) that render the prompts increasingly illegible. Genuine engagement slows this deterioration; evasion accelerates it. No participation prevents it entirely as all paths lead toward illegibility at different rates.

The user may then exit through the gift shop once the text reveals exactly how visitors' words were captured, transformed, and redeployed throughout the text, refusing mystification by making the coercive machinery fully visible. This transparency aligns with critical digital literature practices that challenge hegemonic norms through operational visibility. Drawing on Alain Robbe-Grillet's aleatory performance in N. a pris les dés (1972) and OuLiPo's constraint-based traditions, LaMoS positions interactive fiction's promise of dialogue as a playful systematic reappropriation of reader contributions. Ot is coercive by design and legible by choice.

Biography

Glenn S. Ritchey III is a PhD student in Texts & Technology at the University of Central Florida whose research investigates how to democratize who gets to make meaning through computational literature and constraint-based generative systems as democratic alternatives to corporate AI. Drawing on decolonial epistemologies and open-source principles, Glenn's work positions transparent, rule-governed systems as tools for technological sovereignty. Glenn's creative practice includes Francoism, a 100,000-word Markov-based novel forthcoming from Inside the Castle Press, and Recapitating Massive, a collaborative algorithmic critical edition. Their work has been accepted at ELO 2025 Toronto, SCMS 2026, and the South Asia Research Colloquium in Kolkata. Glenn's academic career is indebted to a background in DIY music and arts and likewise emphasizes building democratic alternatives rather than solely critiquing existing systems. Their research operates at the intersection of computational literature, digital humanities, and decolonial technological studies.
