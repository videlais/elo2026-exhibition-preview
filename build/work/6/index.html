"At the Guggenheim (redux)"

Description

This major expansion and revision of "At the Guggenheim" uses CapCut (an AI video generator) to bring the paintings in the Guggenheim Museum to life and tell the unspoken (and secret) story of the tour guide. Unlike a text-based story, our understanding of the action relies on the visual cues within the animated paintings themselves. How do we "read" them? How can we interpret their narrative cues? Can they tell a story as efficiently, if not more so, than a text-based story? Throughout the online tour, we interact with the paintings on our own, without the help of the tour guide, but we are also subtly manipulated by her curation of the art works. We are granted the illusion of individual interpretation but, the whole time, our judgement is being manipulated, and we are witnesses to an unintentional confession. In the end, our tour is, in fact, (un)supervised. This work uses video effects generated by CapCut (an AI video generator), and then converted into animated gifs using Adobe Photoshop and Adobe Premiere Pro, with additional writing, compiling, and editing using HTML5, JavaScript & CSS. It is playable on desktop, laptop, and portable devices, including phones.

Curatorial Statement

*At The Guggenheim* explores the narrative possibilities of visual interpretation by transforming static paintings into animated sequences that collectively suggest a story hidden beneath the conventions of the museum tour. The resulting experience foregrounds a central question for electronic literature: how do audiences read narrative when textual cues are displaced by image, animation, and interaction? Readers are encouraged to consider the role of mediation in acts of interpretation, highlighting the tension between individual meaning-making and the influence of curatorial authority. By combining museum culture, generative media practices, and visual storytelling, the work offers a reflection on how stories emerge from images, how interpretation is shaped by context, and how digital tools can reanimate existing cultural materials to produce new narrative experiences.
